How do I interpret user needs and implement them into a program? How does creating “user stories” help with this?
How do I approach developing programs? What Agile processes do I hope to incorporate into my future development work?
What does it mean to be a good team member in software development?

As a developer, I interpret user needs by breaking down what someone is trying to accomplish into clear, actionable requirements. Instead of focusing only on technical features, I try to understand the problem from the user’s perspective: what they want to do, why they want to do it, and what success looks like for them.
Creating user stories helps bridge the gap between ideas and implementation because they translate abstract goals into something practical. A well-written user story keeps me grounded in value rather than just code, and it provides a shared language between developers, product owners, and stakeholders. It also makes prioritization 
easier because I can focus on delivering meaningful outcomes instead of building features that may not actually help the user. When approaching development, I try to think iteratively rather than aiming for a perfect final product on the first attempt. Agile methods appeal to me because they allow room for learning, 
feedback, and adaptation as a project evolves. Practices like sprint planning, backlog refinement, and daily standups encourage continuous communication and prevent problems from growing unnoticed. I also value concepts like a clear Definition of Done and regular retrospectives, since they help improve both the product and
the development process over time. In my future work, I hope to combine structured Agile practices with flexibility, allowing me to respond to change while still maintaining a clear direction. Being a good team member in software development means more than just completing assigned tasks. 
It requires communication, accountability, and a willingness to support the team’s overall goals. I believe a strong team member listens to feedback, shares knowledge openly, and stays adaptable when priorities shift. Collaboration is especially important in Agile environments, where success depends on
transparency and trust rather than individual effort alone. By contributing actively, respecting different perspectives, and focusing on shared outcomes, I can help create a development environment where both the product and the people working on it continue to grow.
